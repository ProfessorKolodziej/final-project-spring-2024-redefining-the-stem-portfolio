Areas of Interest

Quantum Physics and Information

Quantum physics and information: One of the most fascinating sources of quantum information lies outside the event horizon of a black hole. In the late 20th century, Hawking’s demonstration that black holes slowly radiate particles spurred a flurry of research regarding the exact quantum nature of these particles. These works led to a deeper investigation into the interplay between black holes and quantum information, leading to new connections in quantum complexity theory and a conjecture about the optimality of black hole scrambling times. But in the absence of a fully quantum theory of gravity, what can we say systematically about the quantum information of Hawking radiation, or how black holes interact with nearby particles? I am interested in developing rigorous information-theoretic models of black holes and using them to reason about their role in quantum information.

Quantum Algorithms

Algorithms for quantum computation—and more generally, quantum information processing—are arguably still at their infancy. There is an enormous range of questions one can ask about quantum algorithms, but here are a few specific questions that I care about:

  1. a. Error correction and compilation:

    For a given quantum physical realization or quantum simulation, what is the best way to protect against errors whilst also ensuring the simplest, e.g. good transversal gate set, algorithm executions?

  2. b. Co-design:

    In the early age of classical computers we boosted computation by combining elements of digital and analog devices, and then co-designing our algorithms to the specific capabilities of that machine. How far can we push that idea in the quantum computing age?

Quantum machine learning

Even in the classical world, AI and ML have reached staggering heights in their practical abilities. How does quantum computation play a role in the frontiers of learning? I am interested in exploring this question from many points of view.

  1. a. When is quantum computing useful in machine learning — are there practical classical learning tasks for which quantum computers have an advantage?

  2. b. Given a quantum machine learning task, what’s the optimal way to actually train a physical quantum circuit to solve it?

  3. c. Are there useful quantum learning tasks that can be achieved by combining quantum and classical computing?

  4. d. What tasks are too hard for even a quantum computer to learn and how do these relate to post-quantum cryptography?
